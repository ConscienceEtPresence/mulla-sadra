The author

مُلَّا صَدْرَا

Ṣadr al-Dīn Muḥammad Shīrāzī

Šīrāz, 979 / 1571-72  —  Baṣra, 1045 / 1635-36

One does not understand a thought without the man who carried it. Here, in broad strokes, is the life of the one posterity called Ṣadr al-Mutaʾallihīn, “the foremost of the divine philosophers” — not as a history of his influences, but to place the time and ground from which his thought arose.

The name

His full name is Muḥammad ibn Ibrāhīm ibn Yaḥyā al-Qawāmī al-Shīrāzī. He is known above all as Mullā Ṣadrā. The honorific Ṣadr al-Mutaʾallihīn was given by the generations after him: literally, the summit of “those who let themselves be divinized” (taʾalluh) — sages whose knowledge is also a path of transformation.

A life, few traces

No detailed biography of Mullā Ṣadrā survives: the essentials are reconstructed in touches. We know he was born in Šīrāz, into an influential family; his father was a high court official, and Ṣadrā, an only son, received particular care. His life traces a movement in three times: formation, retreat, teaching.

979 / 1571-72
Born in Šīrāz. Early studies there, then a first real encounter with philosophy in Qazvīn, before reaching Isfahan, the political and religious heart of Safavid Iran.
1010 / 1601
Return to Šīrāz, probably at his father's death. He finds a hostile climate and clashes with jurists.
~5 years
Contemplative retreat at the village of Kahak, near Qom. There he lays the foundations of his “transcendent wisdom” and begins writing the Asfār.
1040 / 1630
At the height of his maturity he resumes teaching in Šīrāz, at the Khan School, where he will remain to the end.
1045 / 1635-36
He dies in Baṣra, returning from his seventh pilgrimage to Mecca. Where he was buried is unknown.

On the date of death, recent scholarship holds 1045 / 1635-36; an older tradition gave 1050 / 1640.

The era: Safavid Iran and the “School of Isfahan”

Mullā Ṣadrā lived at the height of the Safavid dynasty, under Shāh ʿAbbās I. Iran had become the land of Twelver Shiʿism, and its capital Isfahan an intense intellectual centre. Under the convenient name of the “School of Isfahan” we group the great scholars of that milieu, who joined philosophy, Shiʿi theology and gnosis (ʿirfān).

It was also a time of tension: part of the jurists and literalist currents distrusted philosophy, and Sufism still more. Ṣadrā suffered from this personally; yet he took care to distinguish theoretical gnosis from organized Sufism, and even devoted a critical treatise to it.

His teachers

Two figures of Isfahan formed him, beyond a mere teacher-student bond:

Mīr Dāmād (d. 1040 / 1631)

The most celebrated philosopher of the time, known for reading Ibn Sīnā in the light of Suhrawardī. He inspired Ṣadrā's “synthetic” method. The student later diverged from some of the master's principles, while remaining grateful.

Shaykh Bahāʾī (d. 1030 / 1621)

Bahāʾ al-Dīn al-ʿĀmilī, a first-rank jurist, a scholar and sage of many fields, versed in the spiritual traditions. He was doubtless the source of the mystical reading Ṣadrā made of Shiʿi thought.

(A third name, Mīr Findiriskī, is sometimes cited; but nothing attests that Ṣadrā studied under him.)

His three sources

Three authors marked him more than any other — not to be debated, but as nourishment for his vision: Ibn Sīnā (Avicenna), who gives him the conceptual framework and the model of the system; Suhrawardī, from whom he keeps that thought is fruitful only where intuition lights it; and Ibn ʿArabī, whose mystical vision — the unity of being, the Perfect Human — grounds his transcendent wisdom.

His family

Mullā Ṣadrā and his wife Maʿṣūma had six children: three sons, all of whom became scholars (the eldest, Ibrāhīm, a specialist in law and hadith), and three daughters, married to three of his students. He treated his students as his own family.

His work

More than fifty books and treatises, almost all in Arabic. At the centre, a single summa:

The Transcendent Wisdom in the Four Journeys الحكمة المتعالية في الأسفار العقلية الأربعةThe Asfār, his major work — begun at Qom, completed at Šīrāz, published in nine volumes. Both a personal system and an encyclopedia of Islamic philosophy.
The Divine Witnesses الشواهد الربوبيةA thematic summary of the Asfār.
The Book of Metaphysical Penetrations المشاعرA clear exposition of his ontology; translated into French by Henry Corbin.
The Origin and the Return المبدأ والمعادA digest of his metaphysical cosmology.
The Keys to the Unseen مفاتيح الغيبHis major work of Qurʾanic hermeneutics.
The Treatise of the Three Principles رساله سه اصلHis only work in Persian, written against his detractors.

He also commented on Ibn Sīnā's Šifāʾ, Suhrawardī's Ḥikmat al-išrāq, and the great Shiʿi hadith collection, the Uṣūl al-Kāfī.

The meaning of his project

His system, the ḥikma mutaʿāliya — “transcendent wisdom”, which the West sometimes renders as theosophy — seeks to reconcile philosophy, gnosis and revelation. For him, truth is not reached without intellectual intuition, itself bound to spiritual illumination: intellect and soul work as a single movement. Everything rests on one first intuition — that reality is made of existence, one and graded. That is where this site begins.
